Question 1 — Hook
How will you grab attention on slide 1?
Your first slide is the only one people see before deciding to swipe. Make it count.
Surprising stat
Lead with a number so striking it stops the scroll. E.g. "73% of teens feel lonely every day."
Bold question
Ask something your audience is already wondering. E.g. "Why does nobody talk about this?"
Mind-blowing fact
Drop a fact that sounds unbelievable but is true. E.g. "Honey never expires. Ever."
Controversial take
State something bold that people will agree or disagree with instantly.
Relatable moment
Describe something your audience has felt. "That moment when..." energy.
Direct challenge
Call out your reader. E.g. "If you do this every morning, stop. Here's why."
Before & after promise
Promise a transformation. E.g. "5 slides that changed how I think about stress."
Humor / meme energy
Open funny or absurd. Works great for light topics — risky for serious ones.

Question 2 — Narrative format
How will you tell the story across the slides?
This is your carousel's structure — not what it's about, but how you move through it.
Myth vs. fact
Each slide busts a misconception. Great for health, science, and social topics.
Step-by-step guide
Walk through a process one step per slide. Clear, practical, easy to follow.
Hot take + breakdown
Start with a bold opinion, then defend it slide by slide with reasoning.
Character journey
Follow a character through a transformation. Great for mental health or dharmic topics.
Listicle
"5 things you didn't know about X." One item per slide. Simple and very swipeable.
Two-person conversation
A dialogue — one clueless, one explaining. Adds humor and relatability.
Before & after
Show contrast — life before vs. after a habit or mindset. Visual and emotional.
Meme story
Use meme formats to explain a concept. Best for fun topics.

Question 4 — Text limit (slides 2–4)
How much text will each slide have?
Applies uniformly to slides 2, 3, and 4. If you need more words, you probably need another slide.
A — Headline only
5–7 words max per slide
The visual does the heavy lifting
B — Headline + one line
Headline + max 15-word sentence
Gives context without overwhelming
C — Headline + 3 bullets
Headline + 3 bullets (5 words each)
Ask: could this be 2 slides?
⚠️ Picking C? Seriously ask yourself: can this live across two simpler slides instead?
Question 5 — Visual style
What kind of visuals will you use?
Pick one and stay consistent across all slides. Mixing styles looks messy.
Real photos
Actual photographs. Feels authentic. Use Unsplash or Pexels for free images.
Flat vectors / illustrations
Drawn-style graphics. Clean and modern. Works great with bold color schemes.
Canva animated stock
Canva's built-in animated elements. Eye-catching — use sparingly.
Typography-led
Big, bold fonts ARE the visual. Minimal imagery. High impact when done well.
Meme / screenshot style
Meme formats or phone screenshot aesthetics. Internet-native. Best for humor.
Mixed (photos + graphics)
Photos with illustrated overlays or icons. Needs strong design sense to pull off.
